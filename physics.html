MODULE 1: CORE WAVE PROPERTIES (L1)

Click on any core concept to reveal the simplified definition and relationship.

Key Definitions

Mechanical Waves | Requires Medium

Result from the oscillation of matter. They need a physical substance (medium) like water, air, or rope to travel through. They transport energy, not bulk matter.

Transverse Waves | Example: Light, Water

The particles of the medium move perpendicular (⊥) to the direction in which the wave travels. Think of an 'S' shape on a rope: the highest point is the crest, and the lowest point is the trough.

Longitudinal Waves | Example: Sound

The particles of the medium move parallel (∥) to the direction in which the wave travels. This creates areas of crowding (compression) and spreading out (rarefaction).

The Wave Speed Equation

v = fλ

Speed = Frequency × Wavelength

Wave speed (v) = how fast the wave moves.

The speed at which the energy travels through the medium. It depends only on the medium.

Frequency (f) and Period (T)

Frequency (f, in Hz) is the number of cycles that pass a point per second. Period (T, in seconds) is the time taken for one cycle. They are inverses: T = 1/f.

Wavelength (λ)

The distance over which a wave's shape repeats (e.g., crest to crest). When wave speed (v) is constant, if frequency (f) increases (↑), the wavelength (λ) decreases (↓).

MODULE 2: ENERGY AND BEHAVIOR (L2)

How waves interact with their environment and each other.

Energy & Amplitude

Amplitude is the maximum displacement from rest position (midline).

Key Relationship

Energy carried by a wave is proportional to the square of amplitude (E ∝ A²).

For example, if amplitude doubles (A → 2A), energy quadruples (E → 4E).

Interaction Checklist

1. Reflection

Wave bounces off a barrier. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection (θi = θr).

2. Refraction

Wave changes direction entering a new medium. Speed and wavelength change; frequency remains constant.

3. Diffraction

Wave spreads through openings or around obstacles. Maximum diffraction occurs when λ ≈ opening size.

4. Interference

Two or more waves meet and combine. Constructive → higher amplitude. Destructive → lower or zero amplitude.

MODULE 3: GEOMETRIC OPTICS (L3)

Applying reflection and refraction to mirrors and lenses.

Lens/Mirror Equation

1/f = 1/do + 1/di

f: focal length, do: object distance, di: image distance

Magnification (M)

M = hi/ho = -di/do

  • M negative → Image is real and inverted.
  • M positive → Image is virtual and upright.
  • |M| > 1 → Image is magnified.
  • |M| < 1 → Image is reduced.

Lens & Mirror Quick Rules

Converging (Convex Lens / Concave Mirror)

Forms real (inverted) or virtual (upright) images depending on object's position relative to f and 2f.

Diverging (Concave Lens / Convex Mirror)

Always forms virtual, upright, reduced images. Focal length (f) is negative.

Plane Mirror

Virtual, upright, same size. Distance behind mirror = distance in front. Shows lateral inversion.

Snell's Law / Critical Angle

n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2. Critical angle occurs when refraction = 90°.

MODULE 4: HIGH-YIELD REVIEW FOCUS

Focus on common exam traps and key facts.

Wave Facts to Memorize

  • Sound waves diffract more easily than light due to longer wavelength.
  • Frequency never changes between media; only speed and wavelength change.
  • Pitch depends entirely on frequency.
  • Shallow water wave speed: v = √(g d).
  • Increasing rope tension FT increases wave speed (v ∝ √FT).

Optics Sign Rules

  • Focal length (f): positive for converging, negative for diverging.
  • Image distance (di): positive → real, negative → virtual.
  • Low n → High n: ray bends toward normal. High n → Low n: bends away; risk of TIR.

Standing Waves

Formed by interference of incident and reflected waves. Harmonic number n = number of antinodes.

Fundamental Harmonic (n=1)

Length L = 1/2 λ. Wavelength λ = 2L/n.
